The Time Hierarchy

Every hour your factory loses has a cause and a cost. This framework makes both visible.

The Problem with OEE

Traditional OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness) multiplies Availability × Performance × Quality into a single percentage. This has three problems:

  • It obscures magnitude. A line at 65% OEE could have a 30% downtime problem and negligible quality losses, or moderate losses across all three. You cannot tell which from the number alone.
  • It double-counts. Speed losses on waste units are counted in both Performance and Quality. The multiplicative structure makes this unavoidable.
  • It cannot be valued in pounds. A percentage does not tell you what an improvement is worth. Time does — every lost hour has a financial value that depends on your constraint state.
  • It cannot be summed across products. You cannot add OEE percentages from different SKU runs to get a meaningful total. Time-based measurement can: sum Potential Time and Actual Operating Time across any number of products (provided each has a SKU-specific bottleneck speed) and the ratio remains valid.

Time-based additive decomposition expresses every loss as a duration. Losses are directly comparable, mutually exclusive, summable across products, and convertible to financial impact.

How It Works

Calendar Time is all time that exists. Through a series of subtractions — non-working time, schedule losses, planned downtime, unplanned downtime, speed losses, and quality losses — we arrive at Potential Time: the theoretical minimum time needed to produce your actual good output at maximum bottleneck speed.

Everything between Calendar Time and Potential Time is a loss. The hierarchy classifies every loss into a mutually exclusive category so nothing is double-counted and every loss can be valued.

Key principle: The hierarchy is measured at the bottleneck — the process step that limits throughput. Non-bottleneck steps have excess capacity and are not the constraint.

Which Metrics Matter to You?

Before choosing a metric, identify your constraint state: can you sell more if you made more?

WhoSales-ConstrainedProduction-ConstrainedFormula
Shop Floor Operating Efficiency Operating Efficiency Potential Time / Operating Time
Operations Manager Machine Efficiency Overall Operations Effectiveness Potential Time / Shift Time
Strategic Leadership Output per Labour Hour Total Effective Equipment Performance Output / Labour Hrs or Potential Time / Calendar Time

Machine Efficiency and Overall Operations Effectiveness are the same formula — the name changes with the strategic intent. If weekend working is not possible, use Overall Operations Effectiveness instead of Total Effective Equipment Performance.

The Metrics

Operating Efficiency = Potential Time / Operating Time (Actual)

Shop floor metric. Measures execution within the operational window — Unplanned Downtime, Lost Time Slow Running, and Lost Time Making Waste are in scope. Schedule Loss and Planned Downtime are not (planning decisions). World-class: ≥85%.

Machine Efficiency / Overall Operations Effectiveness = Potential Time / Shift Time

Management metric. Adds Schedule Loss and Planned Downtime to the denominator — holds the operation accountable for how it uses all available shift hours, not just the time allocated to production.

Total Effective Equipment Performance = Potential Time / Calendar Time

Strategic metric for production-constrained environments. Measures total asset utilisation including non-working time. Answers: "how much of the factory's theoretical capacity are we using?"

Output per Labour Hour = Good Output / Total Labour Hours

Strategic metric for sales-constrained environments. Measures how productively labour is deployed. Improving machine efficiency or optimising crew size both increase this metric, reducing cost per unit.

From Time to Money

The framework’s payoff: every lost hour has a pound value.

Production-Constrained
Lost Time × Bottleneck Speed × Unit Marginal Profit = opportunity value.
Every recovered hour produces additional saleable output.
Sales-Constrained
Labour Hours Saved × Fully Loaded Labour Cost = opportunity value.
Recovered time enables fewer people or fewer hours for the same output.

The difference matters enormously. The same 2.5 hours of unplanned downtime per day can be worth £5.4M/yr (production-constrained, valued at throughput margin) or £82k/yr (sales-constrained, valued at labour cost saved) — a 66× difference. Getting the constraint diagnosis right is not optional.

Key Formulas

Potential Time = Good Output / Bottleneck Speed
Up Time = Potential Time + Lost Time Slow Running + Lost Time Making Waste
Operating Time (Actual) = Up Time + Unplanned Downtime
Lost Time Slow Running = Good Output / Run Speed − Good Output / Bottleneck Speed
Lost Time Making Waste = Waste Output / Run Speed
Run Speed = Total Output / Up Time
Verification: Actual Operating Time = Potential Time + Lost Time Slow Running + Lost Time Making Waste + Unplanned Downtime (must balance)
