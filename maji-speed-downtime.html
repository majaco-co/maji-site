Speed-Downtime Trade-off

Interactive calculator for sales-constrained environments. Find the optimal line speed where PPLH (Packs Per Labour-Hour) peaks, accounting for speed-induced reliability and quality degradation.

Understanding the Speed-Downtime Trade-off

In sales-constrained environments, labour efficiency (PPLH — Packs Per Labour Hour) drives profitability. When demand exceeds capacity, every pack your line can produce has full margin value.

Increasing line speed boosts throughput, but typically degrades availability and quality. Run too fast and downtime eats the gains. This calculator models that trade-off to find your optimal operating point.

Baseline Parameters

Parameter Default Value Description
Line Speed 100 packs/hour Current rated speed of the production line
Availability (Uptime) 85% Proportion of scheduled time the line is running
Quality (Yield) 98% Proportion of output meeting quality standard
Operators on Line 4 people Total labour hours consumed per shift
Shift Length 8 hours Duration of production shift

Degradation Sensitivity

When speed increases, both availability and quality typically degrade. Default assumptions:

  • Availability Loss: 5% reduction per 10% speed gain
  • Quality Loss: 2% reduction per 10% speed gain

These rates vary by line type, product, and maintenance standard. Calibrate from your own data for the most accurate results.

How PPLH Is Calculated

Packs Per Labour Hour (PPLH) is the key metric for sales-constrained profitability:

PPLH = (Speed × Shift Length × Availability × Quality) ÷ (Operators × Shift Length)
PPLH = Speed × Availability × Quality ÷ Operators

The optimal speed is where the product of (Speed × Availability × Quality) is maximised — the point where PPLH peaks before degradation outweighs speed gains.

Finding the Optimal Speed

As speed increases, PPLH initially rises. But as availability and quality degrade, PPLH plateaus and then falls. The optimal operating speed sits at the peak of the PPLH curve.

For default parameters (85% availability, 98% quality, 5% availability loss and 2% quality loss per 10% speed increase), the optimal speed is at the baseline — meaning any speed increase degrades more than it gains.
