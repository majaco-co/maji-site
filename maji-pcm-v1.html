Process Control Methodology

A comprehensive guide to strategic buffer placement, mathematical sizing methodologies, and constraint protection in multi-stage production systems.

This page was created by maji, majaco's AI operational excellence tool. maji is in active development, so occasional inaccuracies may appear as the system continues to learn.

The Buffer Paradox

The central insight of constraint-based process control is counterintuitive: strategically increasing specific inventory (constraint protection buffers) while decreasing overall inventory (non-constraint WIP) simultaneously improves both throughput and working capital efficiency.

This is the buffer paradox — the path to less inventory overall runs through more inventory in the right places.

Key principle: Buffer stock in front of the constraint is not waste — it is insurance against throughput loss. The cost of holding it is trivially small compared to the cost of a constrained machine waiting for material.

Mathematical Foundation

Buffer size is calculated using the following inputs:

VariableMeaning
MTBDMean Time Between Disruptions — average time between supply interruptions
MTTRMean Time To Repair/Resolve — average time to restore supply
kProtection factor (typically 1.5–2.5, reflecting variability risk)
Buffer Size = (MTTR / MTBD) × Constraint Rate × k

Worked Example

A packaging line with constraint rate of 100 units/hour, MTBD of 8 hours, MTTR of 1.5 hours, and protection factor k = 2.0:

Buffer = (1.5 / 8) × 100 × 2.0 ≈ 37.5 units minimum; recommended 113 units at full protection

The ROI of this buffer? Exceeding 33,000% annually — because a single hour of constraint starvation costs far more than a day of buffer holding cost.

Buffer Placement Strategy

Not all buffers are equal. Prioritise placement by ROI:

PriorityBuffer TypeTypical ROIPurpose
1 Constraint Buffer 500–1500% Protects the constraint from upstream supply interruption
2 Assembly Buffer 200–600% Protects assembly points from component timing mismatches
3 Shipping Buffer 100–300% Protects customer commitments from internal variation

Monitoring and Dynamic Management

Buffer health is managed through three status zones:

ZoneBuffer LevelStatusAction
Green 67–100% Normal operation No intervention required
Yellow 33–67% Monitor closely Investigate upstream cause; prepare contingency
Red 0–33% Critical risk Immediate escalation; expedite upstream; consider constraint rate reduction

The Paradigm Shift: From Cost to Investment

Traditional inventory management treats all stock as cost to be minimised. Strategic buffer management treats constraint protection stock as an investment with quantifiable returns.

The question changes from "how do we reduce inventory?" to "where should we hold inventory to maximise system throughput?"

Implementation Results

When applied correctly, organisations can expect:

  • 20–40% throughput improvement through elimination of constraint starvation events
  • 15–25% working capital reduction through elimination of non-constraint WIP
  • Improved delivery reliability through shipping buffer protection
  • Reduced firefighting as constraint protection absorbs upstream variation
Six-step implementation: (1) Identify constraint → (2) Size constraint buffer → (3) Remove non-constraint WIP targets → (4) Implement buffer monitoring → (5) Connect upstream to buffer signals → (6) Review and adjust protection factors
