Understanding TSS

What is Training Stress Score?

Training Stress Score (TSS) was developed by Dr. Andrew Coggan as a way to quantify the overall training load of a workout. It takes into account both the intensity and the duration of a ride, giving you a single number that represents how much stress the workout placed on your body.

Intensity Factor (IF) is the ratio of your Normalized Power to your FTP. An IF of 1.0 means you rode at your threshold for the entire duration. Values above 1.0 indicate efforts above FTP, which are possible for shorter durations.

Key benchmark: A 1-hour ride at exactly FTP produces a TSS of 100. This serves as the reference point — a TSS of 50 means roughly half the stress of a threshold hour, while 200 represents twice the load.

How to Use TSS

Daily planning: Use the TSS training load guide above to gauge how much recovery you will need after a workout. This helps you plan the days following a hard session.

Weekly tracking: Sum your daily TSS values to get a weekly TSS. Compare against the weekly guidelines to ensure your training volume matches your goals and experience level.

Long-term trends: TSS feeds into Chronic Training Load (CTL / "fitness"), Acute Training Load (ATL / "fatigue"), and Training Stress Balance (TSB / "form") — the performance management chart used in platforms like TrainingPeaks and WKO.
