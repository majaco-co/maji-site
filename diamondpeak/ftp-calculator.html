Understanding Training Zones

The 7-Zone Coggan Power Model

Dr. Andrew Coggan's power training levels are the standard framework for structuring cycling training with a power meter. Each zone targets a specific physiological adaptation and has a distinct feel, duration, and purpose.

1 Active Recovery

Physiological Adaptation

Promotes blood flow and recovery without adding training stress. Burns primarily fat.

Typical Session

30-60 min easy spin. Coffee ride pace.

RPE

1-2 / 10 — "Could do this all day"

Sustainable Duration

Indefinite

2 Endurance

Physiological Adaptation

Builds aerobic base, increases mitochondrial density, improves fat oxidation. The foundation of all endurance training.

Typical Session

2-5 hour steady ride. Long weekend base miles.

RPE

2-3 / 10 — "Conversational effort"

Sustainable Duration

2-6+ hours

3 Tempo

Physiological Adaptation

Increases muscular endurance and lactate clearance. Often called "no man's land" as it's too hard to recover quickly but too easy for maximum adaptation.

Typical Session

2×20 min or 3×15 min blocks. Brisk group ride pace.

RPE

4-5 / 10 — "Comfortably hard, limited conversation"

Sustainable Duration

1-3 hours

4 Lactate Threshold

Physiological Adaptation

Raises FTP by improving lactate clearance at high intensities. The key zone for time trial and race performance.

Typical Session

2×20 min at FTP, 3×10 min slightly above. 40km TT effort.

RPE

6-7 / 10 — "Hard but controlled, can speak in short phrases"

Sustainable Duration

30-75 minutes

5 VO₂ Max

Physiological Adaptation

Increases maximum oxygen uptake (VO₂max), cardiac output, and the ceiling of aerobic performance.

Typical Session

5×5 min or 4×8 min with equal rest. Hill repeats.

RPE

8-9 / 10 — "Very hard, gasping, want to stop"

Sustainable Duration

3-8 minutes per effort

6 Anaerobic Capacity

Physiological Adaptation

Develops anaerobic energy systems, increases tolerance to high lactate, and improves ability to sustain repeated hard efforts.

Typical Session

8×1 min all-out with 2 min rest. Track-style intervals.

RPE

9-10 / 10 — "Burning legs, maximal effort"

Sustainable Duration

30 seconds - 3 minutes per effort

7 Neuromuscular Power

Physiological Adaptation

Recruits maximum muscle fibres and develops peak power output. Trains the neural pathways for explosive efforts.

Typical Session

6×15 sec max sprints with 5 min rest. Standing start efforts.

RPE

10 / 10 — "Absolute maximum, purely explosive"

Sustainable Duration

<30 seconds

Heart Rate Training Zones

Heart rate zones provide an accessible way to monitor training intensity without a power meter. Two common methods exist: percentage of maximum heart rate (%MHR) and the Karvonen method using heart rate reserve (%HRR).

Karvonen Method vs. % of Max HR

The Karvonen method accounts for resting heart rate, giving a more personalised result. Two athletes with the same max HR but different resting HRs will have different zone boundaries, correctly reflecting their different fitness levels.

Zone Karvonen (%HRR + RHR) % Max HR Method Karvonen Range %MHR Range
Why %HRR is more accurate: A 25-year-old with a resting HR of 45 bpm and a sedentary person with a resting HR of 80 bpm both have a max HR near 195 bpm. Using %MHR, their Zone 2 boundaries are identical. Using Karvonen, the fit athlete's Zone 2 starts at a much lower absolute HR, correctly reflecting that easy effort for them truly is easier. The Karvonen method scales training zones relative to your personal fitness window.
HR lags power: Heart rate responds to effort with a delay of 30-90 seconds. Use heart rate to guide steady-state efforts (endurance rides, tempo blocks) but rely on power for intervals where intensity changes rapidly.

The Polarised Training Model

Research across elite endurance athletes in cycling, running, rowing, and XC skiing consistently shows an 80/20 intensity distribution: roughly 80% of training volume at low intensity (Zones 1-2) and 20% at high intensity (Zones 4-5), with minimal time in the moderate Zone 3.

20% High

Why Zone 3 is "No Man's Land"

Zone 3 (tempo) sits between two stools: it's too hard to allow full recovery between sessions, but too easy to produce the high-end physiological adaptations that come from true threshold and VO₂max work. Athletes who spend too much time in Zone 3 often experience:

Accumulated Fatigue

Moderate-intensity sessions generate enough stress to delay recovery without a proportional fitness gain.

Stagnant FTP

Without enough true high-intensity stimulus, the body has no reason to raise its lactate threshold.

Reduced Volume Capacity

The fatigue cost of Zone 3 reduces how much total training volume you can absorb in a week.

Lactate-Based Training Zones

The lactate response to exercise intensity provides the most physiologically accurate way to define training zones. Two key thresholds divide the intensity spectrum into three distinct domains.

LT1 — First Lactate Threshold

The intensity at which blood lactate first rises above resting levels (~2 mmol/L). Below LT1, lactate production and clearance are balanced. This roughly corresponds to the upper end of Zone 2.

Equivalent Power Zone

Top of Zone 2 / ~75% FTP

LT2 — Second Lactate Threshold (MLSS)

The Maximal Lactate Steady State: the highest intensity where lactate production still equals clearance (~4 mmol/L). Above this, lactate accumulates rapidly and exercise duration is limited. This is effectively your FTP.

Equivalent Power Zone

Zone 4 / ~95-105% FTP

Gold standard but requires lab testing: Accurate lactate threshold testing requires finger-prick blood samples at multiple intensities during a ramp test. This is best done in a sports physiology lab. Power-based zones derived from an FTP test are a practical and reliable proxy for most athletes.

Sweet Spot Training

Sweet spot is the narrow band at 88-94% of FTP where the overlap between tempo and threshold zones creates a uniquely efficient training stimulus. It delivers a near-threshold adaptation at a significantly lower recovery cost.

Why Sweet Spot Works

Sweet spot training maximises time-at-intensity relative to recovery cost. Compared to training at or above FTP, sweet spot sessions allow longer intervals, more total work, and faster recovery between sessions.

The time-crunched athlete's friend: If you have limited training hours, sweet spot intervals (e.g. 2×20 min at 88-94% FTP) deliver the highest training stimulus per unit of time and recovery cost. They are the most efficient way to raise FTP when you cannot train 12+ hours per week.
