Key argument — latent heat avoided: At 3°C the bacon crosses 0°C and thaws. Each pallet absorbs 20.4 kWh of latent heat as the ice melts — this is the single largest thermal energy component (90% of total product load). At -3°C the product never crosses 0°C, so this latent heat exchange is eliminated entirely. The product stays frozen, avoiding £638.8k/year in waste, quality loss, and downstream costs. Even if the 3PL rate changes from £0.53 to £0.59/pallet/day, the net saving is £616.9k/year.
⚠ Key finding — "Free Cooling" Effect: The frozen bacon arriving at -6°C provides 5.66 kWh/pallet/day of cooling to the 3°C store — that's 542% of the store's fabric losses (1.04 kWh). The product more than offsets all heat ingress, meaning the 3PL's net refrigeration duty for this product is effectively zero. The bacon cools the store, not the other way round. This is a powerful argument: the 3PL should not be charging standard chilled storage rates for a product that subsidises their refrigeration.

Model Assumptions & Notes

  • "Free cooling" (current +3°C scenario): When bacon at -8°C enters a +3°C store, it absorbs heat as it warms through 11°C (including crossing 0°C). This reduces the 3PL's refrigeration duty. The latent heat of fusion as ice melts at 0°C is the dominant component — it can offset the store's entire fabric loss, meaning the 3PL's net duty is near-zero.
  • Latent heat is critical: As bacon crosses 0°C (current scenario only), the ice melts and absorbs ~73 kJ/kg of moisture. At 55% moisture × 400 kg/pallet, that's ~73,500 kJ per pallet — dwarfing the sensible heat components. This "free cooling" disappears at -3°C.
  • Proposed scenario (-3°C): Product arrives at -8°C and warms to -3°C — a 5°C rise entirely in the frozen range. No latent heat (no phase change). The product still provides some cooling offset, but far less than at +3°C. The 3PL handles fabric losses minus this smaller offset.
  • Fabric losses: Heat ingress through insulated panels, doors, and infiltration. Proportional to (T_ambient − T_store). A -3°C store has ~40% higher fabric losses than +3°C (assuming 18°C ambient), but the per-pallet cost is modest in a well-insulated facility.
  • COP: Refrigeration efficiency decreases at lower temperatures. Typical COP for +3°C ≈ 3.0–3.5; for -3°C ≈ 2.6–3.0. The difference is small.
  • Fabric loss factor: Calculated from store dimensions, panel U-value, pallet capacity, and an infiltration multiplier. Formula: (surface area × U-value × infiltration) ÷ pallet capacity. The infiltration factor accounts for door openings, air changes, equipment heat, and other non-fabric loads (typically 1.5–3.0×).
  • Commercial rates: If actual 3PL rates are entered, the model calculates both the energy-based "fair" rate and the commercial comparison. The energy duty ratio shows what proportion of the +3°C rate should apply at -3°C.
